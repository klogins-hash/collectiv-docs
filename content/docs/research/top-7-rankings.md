## Detailed Analysis

### 1. MIT Sloan - Business Economics Track (98/100)

**Why This is #1:**

The perfect combination of faculty, structure, and resources for your research.

**Faculty:**
- **Daron Acemoglu** - THE authority on institutional design ("Why Nations Fail")
- **David Autor** - AI displacement and labor markets
- **Esther Duflo** - Field experiments (Nobel laureate)

**Program Structure:**
- Small cohort (3-4 students/year)
- Dual advisors (Economics + Sloan)
- Maximum flexibility for unconventional research
- STEM-designated

**Resources:**
- MIT CSAIL for AI research
- MIT Media Lab for cooperative tech
- Boston/Cambridge ecosystem

**Your Fit:**
- Workday background (enterprise systems)
- AI coding skills
- 13-year commitment
- "Build for 1,000 years" aligns with Acemoglu

**Application Strategy:**
- Target Acemoglu as primary advisor
- Frame as "institutional design for non-extractive systems"
- Emphasize field experiment with real data

---

### 2. Stanford GSB - Economic Analysis & Policy (90/100)

**Why This is #2:**

Best entrepreneurial culture and AI-economics expertise.

**Faculty:**
- **Erik Brynjolfsson** - Stanford Digital Economy Lab, AI + economics
- **Susan Athey** - Tech economics, machine learning
- **Matthew Jackson** - Networks and cooperation

**Program Strengths:**
- Most comfortable with building companies during PhD
- Silicon Valley location for testing
- Basic Income Lab (studies alternative systems)
- Well-funded and interdisciplinary

**Your Fit:**
- Building platform while researching
- AI-native systems focus
- Long-term vision (Stanford values moonshots)

**Application Strategy:**
- Target Brynjolfsson as advisor
- Emphasize Digital Economy Lab fit
- Frame as "transformative AI + institutions"

---

### 3. University of Chicago - Economics (85/100)

**Why This is #3:**

Best field experiments and "does it work?" focus.

**Faculty:**
- **John List** - World's top field experimentalist
- **Richard Thaler** - Behavioral economics (Nobel)
- **Steve Levitt** - Unconventional economic thinking

**Program Strengths:**
- Chicago Experiments Initiative
- "Science of Using Science" program
- Actually builds and tests real programs
- Rigorous empirical methodology

**Your Fit:**
- Building real systems
- "Does it work at scale?" question
- Empirical data generation

**Challenges:**
- Chicago School traditionally pro-market
- May be skeptical of cooperatives
- Need John List buy-in

**Application Strategy:**
- Target John List specifically
- Frame as "field experiment in alternative institutions"
- Emphasize empirical rigor
- Downplay "communitarian" language

---

### 4. UC Berkeley - Economics (83/100)

**Why This is #4:**

Strong on inequality and alternative systems.

**Faculty:**
- **Emmanuel Saez** - Wealth inequality
- **Stefano DellaVigna** - Behavioral economics
- **Yuriy Gorodnichenko** - Institutions

**Program Strengths:**
- Focus on inequality (your system addresses this)
- Bay Area location for testing
- Can work with Berkeley AI Research (BAIR)
- Progressive tradition

**Your Fit:**
- Wealth distribution focus
- Alternative systems openness
- Tech ecosystem access

**Application Strategy:**
- Frame as "wealth inequality solution"
- Emphasize AI + economics intersection
- Position as "empirical test of alternative distribution"

---

### 5. Mondragon University (82/100)

**Why This is #5:**

Perfect philosophical fit, less prestige.

**Unique Advantages:**
- THE cooperative economics institution
- 80+ cooperatives as research lab
- 70 years of cooperative data
- Part-time option (7 years)

**Your Fit:**
- "Build for 1,000 years" (Mondragon = 70 years)
- Communitarian philosophy
- Cooperative structure expertise

**Challenges:**
- Less prestigious in US academia
- Limited AI expertise
- European PhD structure
- Harder US job market placement

**Application Strategy:**
- Emphasize 1,000-year vision
- Position as "bringing AI to cooperatives"
- Consider part-time while building

---

### 6. Cornell Dyson School (78/100)

**Why This is #6:**

Historic cooperative ties, applied focus.

**Strengths:**
- Cornell helped build US cooperative movement
- Applied economics (not just theory)
- Agricultural cooperatives tradition
- Fully funded

**Your Fit:**
- Cooperative economics heritage
- Applied focus (building systems)
- Long-term institutions

**Challenges:**
- Cooperative focus is historical
- Less AI expertise
- Agricultural emphasis

**Application Strategy:**
- Emphasize Cornell's cooperative heritage
- Frame as "bringing cooperatives to digital age"

---

### 7. UMass Amherst - Economics (75/100)

**Why This is #7:**

Most open to heterodox approaches.

**Strengths:**
- Top heterodox economics program in US
- Political economy and alternative systems
- Most comfortable with cooperative economics
- STEM-designated, fully funded

**Your Fit:**
- Cooperative economics comfort
- Alternative institutions focus
- Critique of extraction

**Challenges:**
- Less prestigious
- Limited AI expertise
- Heterodox stigma in mainstream academia
- Smaller network

**Application Strategy:**
- Frame as "heterodox economics meets AI"
- Emphasize cooperative economics
- Consider as safety school
